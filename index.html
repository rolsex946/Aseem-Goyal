The Complete Guide to Knee Replacement Surgery

Everything you need to know — by Dr. Aseem Goyal

January 15, 2026 8 min read Knee Replacement

What is Knee Replacement Surgery?

Knee replacement surgery, also known as knee arthroplasty, is one of the most successful orthopaedic procedures in modern medicine. It involves replacing damaged surfaces of the knee joint with artificial components called prostheses or implants.

The procedure has evolved significantly over the past few decades, with modern techniques and implants offering excellent longevity and function. British-certified implants, like the ones used by Dr. Aseem Goyal, have shown particularly good long-term results.

When is Knee Replacement Needed?

Knee replacement is typically recommended when:

  • Severe osteoarthritis has damaged the knee joint surfaces
  • Pain persists despite conservative treatments (medications, physiotherapy, injections)
  • Daily activities like walking, climbing stairs, or getting up from a chair are significantly limited
  • Knee deformity (bow legs or knock knees) has developed
  • Night pain is disturbing sleep

Types of Knee Replacement

Total Knee Replacement (TKA)

This is the most common type, where all three compartments of the knee are replaced. The damaged ends of the thigh bone (femur) and shin bone (tibia) are resurfaced, and the kneecap surface may also be replaced.

Partial (Unicompartmental) Knee Replacement

When only one compartment is affected, a partial replacement can be performed. This preserves more bone and ligaments, leading to faster recovery and a more natural-feeling knee.

What to Expect: The Surgical Process

The surgery typically takes 1-2 hours. Under spinal or general anaesthesia, the surgeon makes an incision, removes damaged cartilage and bone, and places the implant components. The incision is then closed, and a dressing is applied.

Recovery Timeline

Day 1-3: Hospital stay. You'll begin walking with support and start physiotherapy exercises.

Week 2: Stitches are removed. Mobility improves significantly. Continue home exercises.

Week 4-6: Most patients can walk without support. Return to light activities.

3-6 Months: Full recovery expected. Return to most normal activities including driving, walking, and light exercise.

Conclusion

Knee replacement surgery is a life-changing procedure for those suffering from severe knee arthritis. With modern techniques, quality implants, and expert surgical care, patients can expect excellent outcomes and a return to active living.

5 Myths About Arthritis That Could Be Harming Your Joints

Separating fact from fiction — by Dr. Aseem Goyal

February 8, 2026 6 min read Arthritis

Myth #1: Arthritis Only Affects Older People

One of the most common misconceptions is that arthritis is an "old person's disease." In reality, arthritis can affect people of all ages, including children (juvenile idiopathic arthritis) and young adults. Rheumatoid arthritis, an autoimmune form, often begins between ages 30-50.

Myth #2: Cracking Your Knuckles Causes Arthritis

There's no scientific evidence linking knuckle cracking to arthritis. The sound comes from gas bubbles forming in the joint fluid. However, excessive cracking with pain should be evaluated.

Myth #3: Exercise Makes Arthritis Worse

This is perhaps the most harmful myth. Appropriate exercise is actually one of the best treatments for arthritis. It strengthens muscles around the joint, improves flexibility, reduces pain, and helps maintain a healthy weight. The key is choosing the right type and intensity of exercise.

Myth #4: Arthritis is Inevitable — Nothing Can Be Done

While there's no cure for most types of arthritis, numerous effective treatments exist. From medications and injections to physiotherapy and surgery, we have many tools to manage pain, slow progression, and improve function.

Myth #5: Joint Replacement is the Only Surgical Option

Joint replacement is just one of many surgical options. Arthroscopy, osteotomy, joint fusion, and synovectomy are other procedures that may be appropriate depending on the condition. The right approach depends on your specific situation.

The Bottom Line

Don't let myths prevent you from seeking proper treatment. Early intervention can make a significant difference in your quality of life. If you're experiencing joint pain, stiffness, or swelling, consult an orthopaedic specialist.

Recovering from ACL Injury: A Sports Medicine Guide

Understanding treatment options and rehabilitation — by Dr. Aseem Goyal

March 2, 2026 10 min read Sports Medicine

Understanding ACL Injuries

The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) is one of the four major ligaments in the knee. It's crucial for knee stability, especially during pivoting and cutting movements. ACL tears are among the most common sports injuries, affecting athletes in sports like football, basketball, skiing, and cricket.

How Does an ACL Tear Happen?

Most ACL injuries occur without contact, typically from:

  • Sudden stops or changes in direction
  • Landing awkwardly from a jump
  • Pivoting with the foot planted
  • Overextending the knee

Diagnosis

A thorough clinical examination followed by MRI is the gold standard for diagnosing ACL tears. At Prime Healthcare, we can initiate the diagnostic process immediately with on-site digital X-ray to rule out fractures.

Treatment Options

Non-Surgical Management

Partial tears in patients with low physical demands may be managed conservatively with bracing and physiotherapy. However, this is the exception rather than the rule for active individuals.

Surgical: ACL Reconstruction

For most patients, especially young and active individuals, surgical reconstruction is recommended. The procedure uses a graft (from your own tissue or donor) to replace the torn ligament.

Rehabilitation Timeline

Week 1-2: Reduce swelling, regain range of motion, protected weight bearing.

Week 3-6: Progressive weight bearing, stationary bike, strengthening exercises.

Month 2-3: Walking normally, increased strengthening, balance training.

Month 4-6: Light jogging, sport-specific drills, progressive agility.

Month 6-9: Return to sports (with surgeon clearance).

Key Takeaway

ACL injuries are serious but treatable. With proper surgical care and dedicated rehabilitation, most athletes can return to their pre-injury level of activity. The key is early evaluation and a structured treatment plan.

Desk Job Back Pain? How to Protect Your Spine at Work

Practical tips for a healthier back — by Dr. Aseem Goyal

March 20, 2026 5 min read Spine Health

The Modern Epidemic of Back Pain

Sitting has been called "the new smoking" — and for good reason. Prolonged sitting, especially with poor posture, is one of the leading causes of chronic back pain in working adults. As an orthopaedic surgeon, I see patients daily whose back problems stem largely from their desk jobs.

5 Tips to Protect Your Spine

1. Optimize Your Workspace

Your chair should support your lower back, feet should be flat on the floor, and the top of your monitor should be at eye level. Invest in an ergonomic chair if possible.

2. Take Movement Breaks

Stand up and move for at least 5 minutes every hour. Set a reminder if needed. Even short walks to the water cooler help.

3. Practice Proper Posture

Sit with your back against the chair, shoulders relaxed, and avoid slouching forward. Think of a string pulling the top of your head upward.

4. Strengthen Your Core

A strong core supports your spine. Include exercises like planks, bridges, and bird-dogs in your routine 3-4 times per week.

5. Stay Hydrated

Spinal discs are largely water. Staying hydrated helps maintain disc health and flexibility.

When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention if your back pain:

  • Persists for more than 2 weeks despite self-care
  • Radiates down your leg (sciatica)
  • Is accompanied by numbness or weakness
  • Affects your bladder or bowel function (emergency)
  • Is associated with unexplained weight loss or fever
